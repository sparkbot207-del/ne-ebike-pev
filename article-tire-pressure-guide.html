Tire Pressure Guide

Finding the right PSI for comfort, grip, and efficiency

Why Tire Pressure Matters

Tire pressure affects everything: comfort, grip, rolling resistance, puncture resistance, and battery range. There's no single "perfect" pressure — it depends on your weight, tire size, terrain, and priorities.

Finding Your Baseline

Step 1: Check the Tire Sidewall

Every tire has a recommended pressure range printed on the sidewall. Example: "40-65 PSI" or "2.8-4.5 BAR"

Step 2: Consider Your Weight

Heavier riders need higher pressure:

  • Under 150 lbs: Lower third of the range
  • 150-200 lbs: Middle of the range
  • Over 200 lbs: Upper third of the range

E-bikes are heavier than regular bikes, so add 5-10 PSI to what you'd run on a regular bike.

Step 3: Factor in Cargo

Commuting with panniers or cargo? Add 5-10 PSI. The extra weight needs more support.

Pressure by Tire Type

Road/Commuter Tires (700c, 28-35mm)

  • Typical range: 60-100 PSI
  • Higher pressure = less rolling resistance, harsher ride
  • Sweet spot for most: 70-85 PSI

Hybrid/Gravel Tires (700c, 35-45mm)

  • Typical range: 40-70 PSI
  • More volume = lower pressure needed
  • Sweet spot: 45-60 PSI

Fat Tires (4"+)

  • Typical range: 5-20 PSI
  • Yes, really that low
  • For sand/snow: 5-8 PSI
  • Hardpack/pavement: 12-20 PSI

Mountain/Plus Tires (2.4"-3.0")

  • Typical range: 20-35 PSI
  • Trail riding: 22-28 PSI
  • Pavement: 30-40 PSI

The Comfort vs. Efficiency Trade-off

Higher Pressure

  • Less rolling resistance (better range/speed)
  • Harsher ride, more vibration
  • Less grip on loose/wet surfaces
  • More puncture resistant

Lower Pressure

  • More comfortable (tire absorbs bumps)
  • Better grip and traction
  • Slightly reduced range
  • Risk of pinch flats if too low

Common Mistakes

Running Too Low

Signs you need more air:

  • Tire feels squishy when you press it
  • Bike feels sluggish
  • Rims hitting bumps (pinch flat risk)
  • Sidewall flexing visibly

Running Too High

Signs you need less air:

  • Harsh, jarring ride
  • Bouncing over bumps instead of absorbing
  • Less grip on corners
  • Skipping over rough pavement

Maintenance Schedule

Weekly

Check pressure with a gauge. Tires naturally lose 1-2 PSI per week.

Before Long Rides

Always check before a big ride or commute.

After Temperature Changes

Air expands when hot, contracts when cold. A tire at 50 PSI in your 70°F house might drop to 45 PSI in 30°F weather.

Tools You Need

Floor Pump with Gauge

Essential. Hand pumps work in emergencies but a floor pump makes weekly checks easy.

Presta vs. Schrader

  • Schrader: Same as car tires, wider valve
  • Presta: Narrower, requires unscrewing the tip before inflating

Know which your bike has; most pumps handle both.
